<div class="dablink">This article is about the statistical technique.  For the programming technique, see <a href="/wiki/Static_code_analysis" title="Static code analysis">static code analysis</a>.</div>
<p><b>Static analysis</b>, <b>static projection</b>, and <b>static scoring</b> are terms for simplified analysis wherein the effect of an immediate change to a system is calculated without respect to the longer term response of the system to that change. Such analysis typically produces poor correlation to empirical results.</p>
<p>Its opposite, <a href="/wiki/Dynamic_scoring" title="Dynamic scoring">dynamic analysis</a> or dynamic scoring, is an attempt to take into account how the system is likely to respond to the change. One common use of these terms is budget policy in the United States<sup id="cite_ref-AEA_0-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-AEA-0"><span>[</span>1<span>]</span></a></sup>, although it also occurs in many other statistical disputes.</p>

<h2><span class="editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=Static_analysis&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a>]</span> <span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span></h2>
<p>The term was used in 1977 in an international academic journal<sup id="cite_ref-PubEcon-Tax_1-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-PubEcon-Tax-1"><span>[</span>2<span>]</span></a></sup>, in a discussion of tax policy. In recent years, it has become very common in academic, business and political discussions of US government economic policy.</p>
<h2><span class="editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=Static_analysis&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Examples">edit</a>]</span> <span class="mw-headline" id="Examples">Examples</span></h2>
<p>A famous example of static analysis comes from <a href="/wiki/Overpopulation" title="Overpopulation">overpopulation</a> theory. Starting with <a href="/wiki/Thomas_Malthus" title="Thomas Malthus" class="mw-redirect">Thomas Malthus</a> at the end of the 18th century, various commentators have projected some short-term population growth trend for years into the future, resulting in the prediction that there would be disastrous overpopulation within a generation or two. Malthus himself essentially claimed that British society would collapse under the weight of overpopulation by 1850, while during the 1960s the book <i><a href="/wiki/The_Population_Bomb" title="The Population Bomb">The Population Bomb</a></i> made similar dire predictions for the US by the 1980s.</p>
<p>Similarly, some scientists used a short-term trend of temperature declines during the 1930s to theorize that the world would be in an <a href="/wiki/Ice_age" title="Ice age">ice age</a> by 1978. As with the overpopulation theories, the projection was less accurate than a roll of the dice because it didn't take into account how factors interact, nor how a short-term trend was being treated like a long-term trend.</p>
<p>For economic policy discussions, predictions that assume no significant change of behavior in response to change in incentives are often termed static projection (and in the US Congressional Budget Office, "static scoring"). By contrast, <i>dynamic scoring</i> refers to projections based on historical response of population to the effect of economic policy changes such as tax increases or cuts.</p>
<p>Typically, static analysis works for very simple systems: for example, how fast snow is accumulating in what is thought to be the mid-point of a blizzard. But even then it must be tempered with rationalityguess how much longer the storm might actually last, rather than assuming that snow will fall continually for the next sixty years, and project the average of the storm so far, rather than plotting the curve of its growth as if that will continue to increase for the second half.</p>
<p>However, when applied to dynamically responsive systems, static analysis tends to produce results that are not only incorrect but opposite in direction to what was predicted, as shown in the following applications.</p>
<h2><span class="editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=Static_analysis&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Applications">edit</a>]</span> <span class="mw-headline" id="Applications">Applications</span></h2>
<h3><span class="editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=Static_analysis&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Economic Policy">edit</a>]</span> <span class="mw-headline" id="Economic_Policy">Economic Policy</span></h3>
<p>Presidents from <a href="/wiki/John_F_Kennedy" title="John F Kennedy" class="mw-redirect">John F Kennedy</a><sup id="cite_ref-JFK-paradox_2-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-JFK-paradox-2"><span>[</span>3<span>]</span></a></sup> to <a href="/wiki/Ronald_Reagan" title="Ronald Reagan">Ronald Reagan</a> <sup id="cite_ref-Reagan-Farewell_3-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Reagan-Farewell-3"><span>[</span>4<span>]</span></a></sup> were explicit in their recognition of the apparent paradox that while static scoring would suggest a change in taxes will produce revenue change commensurate with the tax, in reality revenue tends to change in the <i>opposite</i> direction, due to the response of those affected.</p>
<p>The empirical evidence for this is available but not widely discussed. Several references, both from economic<sup id="cite_ref-WSJ-outcomes_4-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-WSJ-outcomes-4"><span>[</span>5<span>]</span></a></sup> and political<sup id="cite_ref-JEC-Lessons_5-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-JEC-Lessons-5"><span>[</span>6<span>]</span></a></sup> sources, draw on data demonstrating the difference between static scoring of a tax policy change, and the actual result.<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6"><span>[</span>7<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=Static_analysis&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Technological singularity">edit</a>]</span> <span class="mw-headline" id="Technological_singularity">Technological singularity</span></h3>
<div class="rellink relarticle mainarticle">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Technological_singularity" title="Technological singularity">Technological singularity</a></div>
<p>Some have criticized the notion of a <a href="/wiki/Technological_singularity" title="Technological singularity">technological singularity</a> as an instance of static analysis: <a href="/wiki/Accelerating_change" title="Accelerating change">accelerating change</a> in some factor of information growth, such as <a href="/wiki/Moore%27s_law" title="Moore's law">Moore's law</a> or computer intelligence, is projected into the future, resulting in <a href="/wiki/Exponential_growth" title="Exponential growth">exponential growth</a> or <a href="/wiki/Hyperbolic_growth" title="Hyperbolic growth">hyperbolic growth</a> (to a singularity), that suggest that everything will be known by a relatively early date.</p>
<h3><span class="editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=Static_analysis&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Satire: safety razors">edit</a>]</span> <span class="mw-headline" id="Satire:_safety_razors">Satire: safety razors</span></h3>
<p>A satire on this idea has been presented using the development of <a href="/wiki/Safety_razor" title="Safety razor">safety razors</a>: After their invention, all safety razors were single-bladed for 70 years. Then the first double-bladed razor was introduced. It only required 15 years for a third blade to be added, and then one year for the fourth and fifth. Fitting these five data points to a hyperbolic curve produced the prediction that within nine years of the calculationby the year 2015safety razors would have an infinite number of blades.<sup id="cite_ref-Economist-blades_7-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Economist-blades-7"><span>[</span>8<span>]</span></a></sup></p>

				<p><b>Symbolic computation</b> or <b>algebraic computation</b>, relates to the use of <a href="/wiki/Machines" title="Machines" class="mw-redirect">machines</a>, such as <a href="/wiki/Computers" title="Computers" class="mw-redirect">computers</a>, to manipulate <a href="/wiki/Mathematics" title="Mathematics">mathematical</a> <a href="/wiki/Equation" title="Equation">equations</a> and expressions in <a href="/wiki/Symbol" title="Symbol">symbolic</a> form, as opposed to manipulating the approximations of specific <a href="/wiki/Numerical_analysis" title="Numerical analysis">numerical</a> quantities represented by those symbols. Such a system might be used for symbolic <a href="/wiki/Symbolic_integration" title="Symbolic integration">integration</a> or <a href="/wiki/Derivative" title="Derivative">differentiation</a>, substitution of one expression into another, simplification of an expression, etc.</p>
<p>Symbolic computation is also sometimes referred to as <b>symbolic manipulation</b>, <b>symbolic processing</b>, <b>symbolic mathematics</b>, or <b>symbolic algebra</b>, but these terms also refer to non-computational manipulation.</p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Software" title="Software" class="mw-redirect">Software</a> applications that perform symbolic calculations are called <a href="/wiki/Computer_algebra_system" title="Computer algebra system">computer algebra systems</a>.</p>
